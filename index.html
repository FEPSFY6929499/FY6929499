Abstract

Helicopters are unique aircraft that rely on rotary blades to generate lift, enabling vertical take‑off, hovering, and multidirectional flight. This paper explores the fundamental aerodynamics of helicopter flight by examining the four main forces acting on the aircraft: lift, weight, thrust, and drag. Lift is explained through Bernoulli’s principle, highlighting how pressure differences across rotor blades allow helicopters to rise and hover. Weight is addressed in terms of gravity and the importance of lightweight materials such as aluminium alloys and composites to improve efficiency. Thrust is discussed as the angled component of lift that enables forward, backward, and lateral movement, while drag is analysed in its parasitic and induced forms, with engineering solutions to reduce resistance. Real‑world applications, including emergency medical evacuations and search‑and‑rescue missions, demonstrate the versatility and importance of helicopters in critical situations. The discussion concludes that balancing these aerodynamic forces through design and material innovation ensures helicopters remain efficient, stable, and indispensable in modern aviation.
